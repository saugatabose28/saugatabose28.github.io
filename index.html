  3. Binary Bomb Defusal
    The “Binary Bomb Defusal” project presented a unique challenge that required a deep understanding of binary programs and a keen eye for detail. The task involved a binary bomb program with multiple phases, each requiring specific input to proceed. The stakes were high, as any incorrect input would result in a virtual ‘explosion,’ and there was a limited time to defuse the bomb. To tackle this challenge, we utilized various strategies and tools. We employed debugging tools like gdb to gain insights into the program’s operation. A crucial part of the process was understanding and interpreting assembly code, which allowed us to decipher the requirements for each phase of the bomb program.
    We also applied control constructs analysis to understand the flow of the program better and predict its behavior based on different inputs. One of the significant challenges we faced was potential buffer overflow exploits, which we addressed by carefully analyzing the program and ensuring that our inputs did not exceed the allocated memory.
    This project was not just about defusing a binary bomb; it was about enhancing our debugging skills for real-world scenarios. It emphasized problem-solving and critical thinking abilities, skills that are invaluable in the field of computer science. Through this project, we learned to navigate complex challenges and deliver solutions under pressure.

  4. CyberSec Innovation: Entropy-Driven Attack Detection & Web Tracking Solutions
    We meticulously analyze server-log data, harnessing entropy analysis to unearth attack methodologies and strengthen network security. Isolation Forest takes the lead, outperforming Local Outlier Factor in speed and accuracy for detecting outliers. Identifying attack anomalies hinges on spotting non-standard services and telnet requests. The pivotal role of entropy in attack detection cannot be overstated; it facilitates the identification of anomalies by assessing combinations of IP addresses and ports. Yet, detecting attacks with similar IP addresses and ports poses challenges, underlining the need for diverse models and comparative analysis for effective anomaly detection.
    Within the realm of machine learning, our mission is to pioneer a model differentiating between functional and tracking JavaScript codes. We leverage TF-IDF for feature extraction and meticulous experimentation to elevate web tracking security. Our toolkit comprises three models: One-Class SVM, K-Means (k=2), and SVC, representing PU Learning and Supervised Learning for thorough evaluation. We apply TF-IDF to both functional and tracking JavaScript files and meticulously partition the data into training and testing sets. One-Class SVM, initially set with 50% contamination, delivers a 56% AUC, further enhanced to 61% AUC with contamination adjustments. K-Means (k=2) yields a more modest AUC of 41% compared to OCSVM. However, SVC emerges as the star player, displaying exceptional results (99.7% AUC, 99.5% TPR, 0% FPR), which may initially hint at overfitting but is confirmed otherwise through cross-validation. This underscores the pivotal role of classifier selection, with supervised learning, particularly SVC, demonstrating its mettle in delivering accuracy and task effectiveness.

  8. Hate Speech Detection: Binary vs One Class-V1
    I conduct extensive experimentation involving hate speech detection models and identify a prevalent issue in existing models—a tendency to treat the problem as a binary detection model despite the dataset's imbalanced nature. Non-hate speeches contribute significantly to the dataset, making hate speech's definition challenging when compared to cyberbullying, racism, and related domains. To address this, I engineer several models, including SVM-based, LSTM-based, and CNN-based models, utilizing fast text word embedding and GloVe word embedding while still framing the problem as binary. Subsequently, I devise a novel approach, reframing the problem as the construction of a single-class model focused exclusively on detecting hate speech. The model is exclusively trained on hate class data, leveraging deep-trained features. Performance evaluation is comprehensive, utilizing four publicly accessible datasets: Davidson, SemEval-2019 (Task-5, Subtask-A), HASOC-2019 (Sub-task B), and Stormfront.
    My implementation predominantly employs MATLAB, leveraging its Deep Learning Toolbox as the primary framework.

  1. LexiFusedNet: A Unified Approach for Imbalanced Short-text Classification using Lexicon-based Feature Extraction, Transfer Learning and One Class Classifiers
    Saugata Bose, Guoxin Su
    Summary: By merging lexicon-driven feature extraction with a transfer learning-oriented deep neural network, significant improvements are observed. This lexicon approach assigns significance to key terms in brief texts, and the subsequent fine-tuning harnesses this weighted information. By bolstering contextual word embeddings with these lexicon values, the text's interpretability is enriched. Techniques such as OCSVM or SVDD, under the one-class classification loss umbrella, pinpoint anomalies based on word significance. Comprehensive tests across four standard datasets confirm superior results in classifying short texts, effectively tackling challenges posed by data disparities, context constraints, and noise.
    PKAW 2023 | full paper

    e-Science 2023 | full paper

  3. Deep One-Class Fine-Tuning for Imbalanced Short Text Classification in Transfer Learning
    Saugata Bose, Guoxin Su, Li Liu
    Summery: The abundance of user-generated online content has presented significant challenges in handling big data. One challenge involves analyzing short posts on social media, ranging from sentiment identification to abusive content detection. Despite recent advancements in pre-trained language models and transfer learning for textual data analysis, the classification performance is hindered by imbalanced data, where anomalous data represents only a small portion of the dataset. To address this, we propose Deep One-Class Fine-Tuning (DOCFT), a versatile method for fine-tuning transfer learning-based textual classifiers. DOCFT uses a one-class SVM-style hyperplane to encapsulate anomalous data. This approach involves a two-step fine-tuning process and utilizes an alternating optimization method based on a custom OC-SVM loss function and quantile regression. Through evaluations on four different hate-speech datasets, we observe that significant performance improvements can be achieved by our method.
    ADMA 2023 | full paper

  4. Deep One-Class Hate Speech Detection Model
    Saugata Bose, Guoxin Su
    Summery: Hate speech detection for social media posts is considered as a binary classification problem in existing approaches, largely neglecting distinct attributes of hate speeches from other sentimental types such as “aggressive” and “racist”. As these sentimental types constitute a significant major portion of data, the classification performance is compromised. Moreover, those classifiers often do not generalize well across different datasets due to a relatively small number of hate-class samples. In this paper, we adopt a one-class perspective for hate speech detection, where the detection classifier is trained with hate-class samples only. Our model employs a BERT-BiLSTM module for feature extraction and a one-class SVM for classification. A comprehensive evaluation with four benchmarking datasets demonstrates the better performance of our model than existing approaches, as well as the advantage of training our model with a combination of the four datasets.
    LREC 2022 | full paper

    Before 2022

  5. A framework for detecting external plagiarism from monolingual documents: use of shallow NLP and N-gram frequency comparison
    Ritambhra Korpal, Saugata Bose (co-first author)
    Summery: In response to the increasing prevalence of copy-paste practices leading to plagiarism, this paper explores an initiative that combines Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques and supervised machine learning algorithms to automatically detect plagiarized texts. The primary focus is on constructing a framework capable of successfully identifying external plagiarism within monolingual texts. The framework utilizes an n-gram frequency comparison approach and is built upon 120 characteristics extracted through NLP preprocessing. Filter metrics are applied to select the most relevant characteristics, followed by the use of a supervised classification learning algorithm to categorize documents into four levels of plagiarism. The evaluation, based on a confusion matrix, demonstrates a remarkable accuracy score of 89% with minimal false positives and false negatives, signifying the efficacy of our plagiarism detection framework.
    ICTCS 2016 | full paper
